CHILLY

Introduction

The chili pepper (also chile, chile pepper, chilli pepper, or chilli[3]), from Nahuatl chÄ«lli, is the fruit of plants from the genus Capsicum which are members of the nightshade family, Solanaceae.[4] Chili peppers are widely used in many cuisines as a spice to add heat to dishes. The substances giving chili peppers their intensity when ingested or applied topically are capsaicin and related compounds known as capsaicinoids.

GROWING CONDITION

We need a warm climate and sunny position. Requires moisture-retentive soil (clay soil so that more water is retained). 18 - 21 ℃ is the ideal temperature.

WHAT DO THEY LOOK LIKE

Chilli plants grow from small to medium sized bushes from knee high to two metres/six feet tall. How big they get depends on the species and variety.

SOWING AND WATERING

Best Time for sowing is February to April. If you want very hot chillies, then in January as it needs a Long summer to grow well. Transplant the seedlings individually into 9-10 cm pots. Chillies cannot withstand a lot of water. Hence, heavy rain and stagnated water could spoil your plant. Make gaps in your soil and holes at the bottom of your pot to make sure it is not over-watered.

PICKING

Different varieties are picked at different stages of their development. Fruits that start yellow or green generally ripen to red, though green chillies will sometimes ripen to orange or yellow, it all depends on the variety. Usually, and regardless of the colour, once they have filled out and become firm crisp and glossy they can be picked. Experiment by picking one to see if it has all it's heat and flavour. The sooner you pick the more the plant will produce so even if you don't need them at the time you should pick them and keep them in the freezer until you do.

TAKING GOOD CARE OF YOUR PLANT

Once the root fills 9-10 cm pots, transfer then in 20-23 cm pots of good compost. Pinch out growing tips when plants reach about 15-20cm (6-8in) high to encourage bushy growth and better cropping. Pollination does need to occur to get fruits. Rub the bristles of a small paint brush, or your little finger, gently over the flower heads to ensure that pollination occurs. Give at least 2 inches of water per week once the fruits have appeared, spray rather than water at the base to prevent over watering.
