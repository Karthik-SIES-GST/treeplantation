ALOE VERA

Aloe vera is a succulent plant species of the genus Aloe. An evergreen perennial, it originates from the Arabian Peninsula, but grows wild in tropical, semi-tropical, and arid climates around the world. It is cultivated for agricultural and medicinal uses. The species is also used for decorative purposes and grows successfully indoors as a potted plant.

CONDITIONS/REQUIREMENTS

Climate:

The Aloe species are often thought to grow only in warm and dry climates. In fact, however, Aloe vera and many other Aloe species can grow and grow in a variety of climatic environments. These include both desert and alpine sites. However, a suitable climate for Aloe to become efficient crops is that of tropical and subtropical regions.

Soil:

All members of the Aloe genus require sandy or gravelly soil with very good drainage to reduce the risk of root rot and nutritional deficiencies. When planted in containers, aloe vera plants do well in a mixture of commercial potting mix with extra sand, granite grit or perlite added. This plant naturally occurs in extremely dry and poor soils. It will grow well in many different types of soil, from sandy to moderately fertile loam, as long as it has good drainage. To increase drainage at your planting site, build a mound before planting the aloe, incorporating organic compost into the soil.

STEPS FOR PROPAGATION OF NEW ALOE PLANT

The aloe plant cannot be grown from a leaf cutting, and instead is typically propagated by detaching younger clone plants from the base of the adult plant or from the joint root system.

1)Let your adult aloe plant grow to fill its container. While any healthy aloe plant has a chance to produce younger plants, or "pups", this is most likely to happen when the adult plant has reached the boundaries of its container.

2)Wait until young plants emerge. Your Aloe vera plant should start to produce "pups", which are clones of itself that share some of the mother plant's root system and may be attached to the base as well.

3)Let the young plants grow to sufficient size. The young plants will do best if you wait until they are a little larger and mature enough to have a few roots of their own (at least 3 inches (7.5 cm) tall, and preferably 5 inches (12.5 cm).)

4) Use a sharp, clean knife to remove the young plant. Clear away the dirt at the base of the pup to see whether it is attached to the mother plant. If it is, cut it away, making sure to keep the young plant attached to its roots if any are present.

5) Leave cut plants in the air for a couple days. Instead of planting the new aloe immediately, you may wish to allow the plant to form a callus over the knife cut.
6)Plant in its own container and support. Place the young plant on top of well-draining soil, without burying the leaves.

7)Mist once every few days if the plant has no roots. Before the roots have grown in, do not water the plant.

8)Water sparingly after roots set in.
